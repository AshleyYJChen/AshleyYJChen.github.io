Campus Bins Redesign

Big Belly trash bin redesign proposal for UCSD campus bins based on research.




Overview

Utilized the double diamond design process and research methods to redesign the Big Belly interface for UCSD students to make sorting trash quicker while also increasing the rate of accurate recycling.

Challenge

Despite the existing interface on Big Bellies to show what is recyclable or not, the unnoticed signifier among the university students leads to the inaccurate sorting of items.
After a dot voting session, our general consensus was to redesign the Big Belly Trash Bins on campus. This idea came about because we have noticed that the recycle bin is often filled with non-recyclable items.




How might we create a persuasive bin system to make sorting more accurate?




Solution at a Glance

A redesigned interface additional features is a realistic prototype for students.


It has fully colored bins, obvious, relevant guide labels, a light post, a scanner, and a liquids chute. It is able to convey accurate and quick information to the user, even at night, to throw their item in the correct bin.





Design Process

We followed the double diamond design process to redesign the Big Bellies







User Research

Finding the Causes
In order to find out the reasons that hindered students from accurately disposing items in trash or recycle, field observations, interviews, and card sorting were done.




Field Observations
Took note of the natural behavior of students and their process of disposal.

Interviews
Spoke with people after they threw away an item to understand their though process and thoughts on the current bin designs.

Card Sorting
Utilized the modified Delphi card sorting technique to see the mental models students as they sort items into trash, recycle, or other.




Analysis

Insights we found were

  • Time is important. People don't spend much time on disposing an item; a factor we didn’t consider beforehand.

  • 3/6 people were unsure on sorting filled water bottles and 9/14 inaccurately threw away their items from the interviews.

  • The labels on the bins weren’t noticed 12/14 of the time.



Problem

Big Belly Redesign
After competitive analysis of different trash bins, we decided that a persuasive design and the time taken for disposal are two factors to optimize for the redesign.

Redesign

After many iterations, we combined ideas to create a new Big Belly with a different image that aims to help students make quick and accurate sorting decisions. We included a scanner on the bins to help tell items apart and save time on making decisions and a light post for illumination of the bins at night to see the labels. When throwing out trash, students can easily see the signs and quickly decide the correct bin to dispose of their item.
